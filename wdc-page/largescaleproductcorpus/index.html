Web Data Commons - Training Dataset and Gold Standard for Large-Scale Product Matching

Anna Primpeli
Christian Bizer
Ralph Peeters

We present the WDC Training Dataset and Gold Standard for large-scale product matching. The Full Training Set contains more than 26 million entities describing product offers deriving from 79 thousand websites. We also offer the English subset of the Training Set containing 16 milion offers from 43 thousand websites. Using annotated product identifier information we group the offers of the Full Training Set into 16 million clusters and the offers of the English subset into 10 million clusters. In addition we build a gold standard for evaluating existing matching methods by manually verifying the labels of 2000 pairs of offers. Using a subset of the training corpus and the gold standard we are able to reproduce the results of Mugdal et al. [1] showing that embeddings and deep learning methods outperform traditional symbolic matching methods. In the following sections, we describe the data cleansing procedure for the creation of the Training Set and give profiling information. In addition we describe the creation of the gold standard and provide experimental results using existing matching methods. In the last section of this webpage you can find all the relevant files (Training Corpus, English Subset, and Gold Standard) for public download.

1. Motivation for the WDC training data corpus for large-scale product matching

As the research focus in the area of entity resolution moves from symbolic matching methods to deep neural network methods, large amounts of training data are needed to verify if this family of data-hungry, subsymbolic methods outperforms traditional ones. Existing training corpora contain data that either refer to a small group of unique products or derive from a small number of sources. In addition, large amounts of training data used to evaluate existing subsymbolic matching methods are proprietary and cannot be publicly used. By creating the WDC data corpus for large-scale product matching, we intend to enable the application, evaluation, and comparison of different more or less data hungry integration methods on the product domain.

Popular annotation vocabularies, like schema.org, propose a set of properties to annotate identifiers of offer entities on the web. In addition, google motivates the annotation of product identifiers with properties such as schema.org/Product/sku, gtin8, gtin13, gtin14, and mpn for more enhanced search results. The application of these annotation practices can be considered as inexpensive supervision offered in the web. We profile the adoption of identifier-related schema.org product properties using the Web Data Commons November 2017 schema.org/Product subcorpus, perform a series of cleansing and grouping steps, and create the WDC Data Corpus for Large-Scale Product Matching containing 26 million entity offers grouped into 16 million clusters.

3.Training Dataset Creation

Considering the noisy nature of web data, we perform a series of cleansing steps upon creating the Training Set. During the cleansing process we identify several false annotation practices and develop different strategies to overcome them. Below we provide details about each cleansing step and the effect they had on the resulting corpus in terms of size.

Usage of non-existing schema.org terms

A large number of offer entities are assigned wrong schema.org vocabulary terms such as schema:IndividualProduct/sku and schema:Product/Offer/sku. Despite the wrong vocabulary usage, such terms still reveal identifying information. We capture this by applying the following regex pattern on the properties of the offer entities: .*/(gtin8|gtin12|gtin13|gtin14|sku|mpn|identifier|productID).

Leveraging entity relations

We identify that approximately 20% of the offer entities selected in the previous step have no schema.org descriptive properties like schema:name or schema:description. This originates in the annotation practice of attaching the identifier information to the embedded schema.org/Offer entity rather than to the parent schema.org/Product entity as proposed by google on adding structured data to product pages. An example of such an annotation practice is shown in Figure 1. In order to enrich the empty descriptive properties of the training corpus offers we additionally consider the descriptive properties of their parent items. In the example of Figure 1 we would add to the offer with id KZZ99789 the name property of its parent product entity with the value "Boys Lace Up Camo High Top Trainers". This leads to a reduction of offers with empty descriptive properties to less than 3%.

Listing pages and advertisement offers detection and removal

In this cleansing step we want to remove the offers of our corpus which are part of listing pages or advertisements. We apply a heuristic for detecting listing pages and advertisements based on:

  • Amount of schema:Offer and schema:Product entities per url
  • Distribution of descriptions' length
  • Semantic connection to parent entities with properties like schema:RelatedTo and schema:SimilarTo
  • Number of identifier values
The applied heuristic achieves an F1 score of 94.8% (on the positive class isListingOrAd). For the evaluation we use a manually annotated set of 80 schema:Offer and schema:Product entities of our corpus, which you can download here.

Value normalization and filtering by value length

The next filtering step is to remove offer entities which have been annotated with invalid identifier values. As we do not possess an extensive list of all valid product identifiers, we consider the definitions of the relevant schema.org identifier properties and apply value normalization. First, all non-alphanumeric characters and common prefixes such as ean, sku, and isbn are removed. Considering the suggested length and structure of the identifying properties, we remove the values that after normalization are shorter than 8 characters or longer than 25 characters. Finally, we remove the values that consist only out of alphabetic characters.

ID-Clusters creation

In this step we group the offer entities into ID-Clusters using their identifiers. As it happens that single offers contain multiple alterative identifiers we use this information to merge clusters refering to the same product. This results in 16,193,300 ID-Clusters. We also note that some websites include identifiers refering to product categories, such as UNSPSC number in addition to single product identifiers. For detecting these cases we examing the structure of the identifier co.occurrence graph within each ID-Cluster. We discover that vertices having a high degree (>10) and a low clustering coefficient (<0.2) tend to represent product categories rather thsn single products. Therefore we split these clusters by removing the product category identifiers. This removes 90,073 pffer entities and results into the creation of 199,139 additional ID-Clusters. In addition we create the English Training Set by filtering from the ID-Clusters the offers from pages having the suffixes: com, net, co.uk, and org. ID-Clusters.

Specification Table Detection and Extraction

Based on the work of [Qiu,2015] and [Petrovski,2017] on detecting specification tables in html pages, we apply a table detection heuristic based on the following html table attributes:

  • Ratio of alphanumeric characters
  • Average number of columns per row
  • Count of table children elements
  • Count of images
  • Maximum number of columns
  • Max number of rows
  • Average length of text per row
For evaluating this heuristic we manually annotate 455 html tables found in 17 html pages with the labels "specification" and "non-specification" which you can find here. Based on our gold standard the applied heuristic reaches an F1 of 78% on the positive class which is considered the one with label "specification".

Offer Categorization

We use distant supervision from amazon product data in order to assign product category labels to the offers of our corpus. Using a publicly available amazon dataset of product reviews and metadata, we build lexica containing terms and their tf-idf scores for 26 product categories. We assign to every offer of our corpus the product category whose terms maximize the sum of the tf-idf scores of the overlapping terms. In the case that the overlap is very minimal we assign to the offer the product category "not found". For evaluating our categorization strategy, we use a manually annotated set of 985 offers which you can download here. Figure 2 shows the distribution of the different categories in our gold standard. Our categorization strategy reaches 62.3% Precision@1.

6.Baseline Experiments

We perform a set of Baseline Experiments to showcase the difficulty of the Gold Standards as well as the suitability of the WDC Training set as training data for supervision. These are:

  1. an unsupervised experiment using TFIDF and cosine similarity using different feature combinations.
  2. a supervised experiment using word co-occurrence between product features as classifier input.
  3. a supervised experiment using the Magellan framework with auto generated features based on string similarity.
  4. a supervised experiment using the deepmatcher framework which offers multiple Deep Learning models for product matching.

For our supervised Baseline Experiments we use a subset of the English Training set as training data for the algorithms. It is built by creating positive pairs between offers in the same clusters and negative pairs between offers in different but textually similar clusters (Jaccard similarity of titles). The clusters we use for this are those that have offers in the Gold Standards. For more specific information about the build process please refer to the paper.

All of the supervised experiments apart from deepmatcher use the scikit-learn library implementations of Logistic Regression, Naive Bayes, LinearSVC, Decision Tree and Random Forest. Deepmatcher is built upon PyTorch. In preparation for the experiments we remove non-alphanumeric characters, stopwords (using NLTK), and lowercase everything in the Gold Standards as well as the Training sets.

For all experiments using sci-kit learn, we use Gridsearch or RandomizedSearch with 5-fold cross-validation to optimize model parameters. Once optimal parameters are found, the models are retrained on the full training sets before evaluating on the gold standard.

The deepmatcher experiments are conducted using pre-trained character-based fastText embeddings (on english wikipedia) as well as fastText embeddings trained on the English Training Set. The self-trained embeddings are 300-dimensional, trained for 50 epochs on all words that occur at least twice as well as n-grams up to 3-grams, using the skip-gram model. The deepmatcher models themselves are trained using the default settings, apart from mini-batch size and pos-neg ratio which is manually set per dataset. All models are trained for 15 epochs. Furthermore the deepmatcher implementation requires the training set to be split into training and test set. This test set is used to avoid overfitting to the training set. We split our Training sets with a ratio of 4:1 for this purpose. Note that this essentially reduces the amount of training data available to the deep learning methods compared to the other supervised experiments.

7.Corpus Files - Download

We offer the WDC training data corpus for large-scale product matching of the 26M annotated offers as json objects including the following information for every offer:

  • url: The URL where the annotated offer entity was found (considering the November 2017 Common Crawl Corpus)
  • nodeID: The subject assigned by the WDC Extractor. The combination of url and nodeID can be used as unique keys for every annotated entity.
  • clusterID: The ID of the cluster where the offer belongs.
  • identifiers: A list of all annotated identifier values of the offer entity together with their corresponding schema.org properties.
  • schema.org_properties: A list of all annotated schema.org product descriptive properties such as name, description, brand etc. and their values.
  • parent_NodeID: The node ID of the parent entity of the offer.
  • relationToParent: The relation that connects the offer to its parent entity.
  • parent_schema.org_properties: A list of all annotated schema.org product descriptive properties of the parent entity of the offer.

In addition we offer an English subcorpus by filtering the following public suffixes: com, net, co.uk, us, and org. The English subcorpus contains 16M annotated offer entities.
