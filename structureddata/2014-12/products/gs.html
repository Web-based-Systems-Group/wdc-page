Schema.org Products Categorization

Creating a Gold Standard for Schema.org Product Descriptions
Anna Primpeli
Robert Meusel
Christian Bizer

On this page you find a manually annotated dataset, containing over 9,400 product desriptions retrieved from the latest WDC Microdata extraction of 2014. The descriptions are annotated with the category from the first three hierarchy levels of the GS1 Global Product Catalogue. This means each description has been assigned to three categories, one from each level. The so generated goldstandard can be used to train predictive models in order to assign categories to unseen products.

Contents

1. Motivation

A significant number of online stores use mark up languages as RDFa and Microdata to semantically annotate the product descriptions within their HTML. A reason for this is the consumptions of such annotated data by search engine companies as Google and Yandex, which further improve their search results or their knowledge graph with such structured data. Using Microdata together with the schema.org vocubulary, in particular the classes schema.org/Product and schema.org/Offer is one way to realize this semantic markup. Although the schema for those entities is rather comprehensive, only a few properties are used frequently in the Web. In particular we are interested in the usage of the category property, which is dedicated to contain a textual description of "A category for the item. Greater signs or slashes can be used to informally indicate a category hierarchy." We observed, that even when this property is present, the sites make use of their own categorization schema. This leads to a strong heterogeneity of the available categories in the product descriptions across the Web. Using this information, one is not directly able to gain further insights about the general distribution of categories (e.g. from a given category catalogue) within the data. A solution to this would be the selection of a defined product categorization standard and the assignment of a category label to each one of the existing product entities. This could be achieved with the implementation of classifiers that could predict the most precise category label based on the existing product attributes. For this reason the creation of a gold standard that contains a comprehensive number of randomly chosen annotated schema.org/Product entities together with their manually assigned category labels, is essential. The product related entities constructing the gold standard are a subset of the crawled data as extracted by the Web Data Commons project.

2. Description of the gold standard dataset

For the creation of the gold standard we chose randomly 9,414 product entities deriving from websites of different commercial purposes and origin. Every product was described by the following properties:

  • GeneratedID: A automatic generated identify, unique for each product description.
  • EntityNodeID: The original entity node of the root entity (schema.org/Product). This id is unique in combination with the URL.
  • URL: The URL which embedded the product description.
  • HOST: The host based on the URL.
  • s:name : The name property as defined by schema.org
  • s:description : The description property as defined by schema.org
  • s:brand : The brand property as defined by schema.org
  • Properties : All the properties describing the current product entity
  • s:category : The property as defined by schema.org OR
  • s:breadcrumb : The breadcrumb property as defined by schema.org

3. The GS1 Global Product Catalogue

As it is observed from the table presented in the previous section the current categorization of products is not only rare but it also has a high variance degree. Thus a product catalogue for a unified categorization is needed. For this purpose we initially used the product classification proposed by GS1 Global Product Catalogue (GPC). The version of 01-12-2014 of the GS1/GPC catalogue suggests 6 different levels of categorization. The specified levels start with the industry segmentation and are then divided into classes and families of products. The example presented below derives from the GS1 website and depicts the mapping of a milk product in the first four levels.

4. Creation of the Gold Standard based on GS1/GPC

For the creation of our gold standard we used the three broader classification levels as defined by GS1/GPC namely: segment, family and class. In order to assign a category label to a product entity we first looked at the title of the product. In the case that the title was not sufficient for giving a category label we turned to the description and the website in which the product information was published, which most of the times contained a picture of the described product.

We also removed the products we could not assign to any GS1/GPC category.

5. Mapping between GS1/GPC and Google Product Taxonomie

During the category labelling process we observed some limitations of the GS1/GPC categorization for products such as house amenities, travelling services and job offers. As a result of these limitations 187 product entities could not be categorized. For this reason, we further made use of the Google Product Taxonomie for product categorization which is more focused on the Web. This categorization includes three classification levels. As a first step unique identifiers were given to the Google Product Taxonomie categories of all the three levels and then a mapping between the first two levels and the GS1 categories of the first three levels was attempted. These mappings could be partially implemented as there were no exact equivalences between the two catalogues while in some cases there were 1:n relations between the categories.
